CEO Pay

admin | January 2, 2008 4:37 pm

Breaking News sent by Noni Mausa:

OTTAWA — By 10:33 a.m. Wednesday, Canada’s top 100 CEOs had already earned the same amount of money that will take the average full-time worker all year to make, according to a left-leaning think-tank.

The Canadian Centre for Policy Alternatives (CCPA) said in a report that these corporate elites made $39,998 by mid-morning Wednesday, based on an assumption of nine hours and 33-minutes worth of pay based on an average annual salary of about $8.5 million.

…

The report added that by 1:04 p.m. New Year’s Day, these CEOs had already earned what will take a minimum-wage earner all year to make.

My guess is that American top 100 CEOs didn’t have to wait until Wednesday to bring in what the average American will make over the coming year.

Training Iraqis

admin | January 2, 2008 9:38 am

I was reading this Atrios post and I started to wonder… why exactly does the Iraqi military need any training from the US at this point or going forward? I can understand in 2003, 2004, and maybe in 2005 – when you dismiss much of the existing military, you essentially need to train replacements from scratch. Its especially difficult, we’re told, to train NCOs and the lower echelon of the officer corps – lieutenants and captains.

But by now, the Iraqi military has been in some semblance of combat for almost five years. If five years of actual combat isn’t enough time to produce a generation of capable NCOs and officers who could then train the next generation of Iraqi military, how exactly are American personnel supposed to be useful?

Career Coaches

admin | January 2, 2008 9:25 am

Yesterday I went to a cheese party (they had wine too, but its not a wine and cheese party to a guy who doesn’t drink) at the home a successful photographer for a few hours. Him being a photographer, this being Los Angeles, it was an eclectic crowd. Everything from button down manager types to folks with so many tattoos they could probably not wear shirts and get away with it.

It seemed to me that the fraction of the people who have lost their job, are looking for another job, or are hurting for work among the free-lance types (and to some extent, I’m in that boat myself – work has been slow for a couple of months though I’m confident its about to pick up) is much higher than you’d expect with a 4.x% unemployment rate. But then, I’ve made this observation before so I’m not going to belabor the point.

Which led to this… last night there was some discussion about “career coaches” and whether or not they actually help a person get a job. Opinions ranged. So I was wondering:

1. Do career coaches actually help a person get a job. (And since apparently they charge unemployed people thousands of dollars, that’s the only relevant question?)
2. How does one test that proposition?
3. If career coaches can actually be useful, what are the objective ways to tell the difference between the useful ones and the useless ones? (It strikes me that asymmetric might be a problem, except that the information might not be so asymmetric – perhaps professional career coaches might not have any idea about whether they’re helpful or not.)

Debates and Ron Paul

admin | January 1, 2008 2:56 pm

Its being noted that Fox News plans to leave Ron Paul off the upcoming Republican debate its televising. This despite the fact that candidates that don’t poll as well in Iowa and New Hampshire, but who are more to the liking of the national Republican establishment, will be included, and the New Hampshire GOP wants him there.

Now, I’m no Ron Paul supporter, but this really doesn’t seem right to me. If he is excluded, it should be for failing to meet some objective criteria, and others who fail to meet that same objective criteria should be left out as well.

It wasn’t long ago that many on the right were excoriating Dem candidates for not wanting to appear on a debate on Fox. It looks like perhaps Rep candidates shouldn’t either.
